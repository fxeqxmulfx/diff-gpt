\begin{abstract}
We study autoregressive forecasting of continuous time series via a decoder-only
transformer trained on a derivative-based tokenization. The encoder differentiates
the input, scales by its observed range, and quantizes with a first-order
$\Sigma\Delta$ modulator; the decoder inverts this exactly up to a vocabulary-width
term. We prove two tokenization-level bounds: the achievable cross-entropy loss
grows as $\log V$, and the reconstruction error decays as $1/V$, both
independently of the model. We additionally introduce an ordinal Gaussian
soft-target cross-entropy that turns the bin-wise classification loss into a
distance-aware regression loss over bin indices. On the M4 Hourly short-term
forecasting benchmark, ordinal smoothing reduces sMAPE from $18.58$ to $17.49$
(6\% relative) without any change to the architecture or training schedule.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Most state-of-the-art time-series forecasters are regression models with
specialized inductive biases (patching, inverted attention, channel mixing).
They treat the output as a continuous vector and optimize an $\ell_2$ or
Huber loss. By contrast, large language models are decoder-only transformers
trained with cross-entropy over a discrete vocabulary. The question we address
is whether a pure language-model recipe --- fixed architecture, fixed loss,
autoregressive generation --- is competitive on standard forecasting benchmarks,
once the continuous input has been mapped into a faithful discrete
representation.

The key choice is the tokenization. We describe a derivative-based encoder
with first-order $\Sigma\Delta$ (sigma--delta) quantization that has two
desirable properties: (a) cross-entropy loss at the achievable rate scales
linearly in $\log V$, so vocabulary size has a predictable information-theoretic
cost, and (b) the reconstruction error between a round-trip of the encoder
and decoder is bounded uniformly in sequence length by $\Delta/2 = D/(V-2)$,
where $D$ is the maximum observed first difference. These two bounds are the
tokenization contract --- they hold regardless of what model sits on top.

The rest of the paper: Section~\ref{sec:encoder} describes the encoder and
decoder; Section~\ref{sec:theory} proves the two scaling laws; Section~\ref{sec:loss}
introduces ordinal soft cross-entropy and motivates it from the differential-entropy
bound; Section~\ref{sec:experiments} reports M4 Hourly and ETTh1 results.

\section{Derivative tokenization}
\label{sec:encoder}

Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times F}$ be a multivariate signal with $F$ channels
of length $T$. We fix a derivative order $k \ge 1$ (all results below are for
$k = 1$); define $D_c = \max_{t} |x_{t+1,c} - x_{t,c}|$ per channel (the
\emph{domain of definition}) and per-channel scale
$s_c = (V-2)/(2 D_c)$ where $V \in 2\mathbb{Z}_{\ge 2}$ is the vocabulary size.

\subsection{Encoder.}
For each channel the encoder computes
\begin{align}
y_t &= s \cdot (x_{t+1} - x_t), \qquad |y_t| \le (V-2)/2, \\
u_t &= \mathrm{trunc}(y_t) \in \mathbb{Z}, \\
e_t &= y_t - u_t, \\
E_t &= \textstyle\sum_{s \le t} e_s, \quad R_t = \mathrm{round}(E_t), \quad R_0 = 0, \\
c_t &= R_t - R_{t-1}, \\
q_t &= u_t + c_t + V/2 \quad \in \{0, \dots, V-1\}.
\end{align}
This is a first-order $\Sigma\Delta$ modulator. The truncation residual $e_t$
is accumulated; when the accumulator crosses $\pm\tfrac12$, a unit carry $c_t \in
\{-1, 0, +1\}$ is pushed into the next token. Tokens exiting the range
$[0, V-1]$ due to $c_t$ carry are clipped; Remark~\ref{rem:clip} discusses the
effect.

\subsection{Decoder.}
Given the start $x_1$ and the token sequence $(q_t)$ the decoder reconstructs
\begin{equation}
\hat x_{t+1} = \hat x_t + (q_t - V/2)/s, \qquad \hat x_1 = x_1.
\end{equation}

\section{Tokenization contract}
\label{sec:theory}

Two tokenization-level results, both independent of the model.

\subsection{Reconstruction bound}

\begin{theorem}[reconstruction, $k=1$]
\label{thm:recon}
For any signal with $D = \max_t |x_{t+1} - x_t| > 0$,
\begin{equation}
\|\hat x - x\|_\infty \;\le\; \frac{D}{V-2} \;=\; \frac{\Delta}{2},
\quad\text{uniformly in } T.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The proof rests on two elementary facts about the $\Sigma\Delta$ loop.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:round}
Let $\rho_t = E_t - R_t$. Then $\rho_0 = 0$ and $|\rho_t| \le \tfrac{1}{2}$
for all $t$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Direct: $R_t$ is $E_t$ rounded to the nearest integer.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[per-step cancellation]\label{lem:step}
$u_t + c_t = y_t + \rho_{t-1} - \rho_t.$
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
$c_t = R_t - R_{t-1} = (E_t - \rho_t) - (E_{t-1} - \rho_{t-1}) = e_t + \rho_{t-1} - \rho_t$.
Add $u_t$ to both sides and use $y_t = u_t + e_t$.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:recon}]
The decoded increment is $\hat x_{t+1} - \hat x_t = (q_t - V/2)/s = (u_t + c_t)\Delta$.
The true increment is $x_{t+1} - x_t = y_t \Delta$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:step}
their difference is $(\rho_{t-1} - \rho_t)\Delta$. Summing from $t = 1$ to $T-1$
telescopes:
\begin{equation}
\hat x_T - x_T \;=\; \Delta \sum_{t=1}^{T-1}(\rho_{t-1} - \rho_t)
             \;=\; \Delta\,(\rho_0 - \rho_{T-1})
             \;=\; -\rho_{T-1}\,\Delta,
\end{equation}
using $\hat x_1 = x_1$ and $\rho_0 = 0$. Lemma~\ref{lem:round} gives
$|\rho_{T-1}| \le \tfrac12$. The same argument on any prefix yields the
uniform-in-$t$ bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[scaling]
$\|\hat x - x\|_\infty = O(D/V)$. Vocabulary doubling halves the reconstruction
error.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}[clipping]\label{rem:clip}
Since $|y_t| \le (V-2)/2$ and $|c_t| \le 1$, we have $q_t \in [0, V] \cup \{-1\}$.
Saturation to $\{-1, V\}$ is clipped and introduces at most one extra $\Delta$
per clipped step. Because $|y_t|$ attains its maximum only on a finite (and
typically empty) set of $t$ where the carry direction also aligns, the total
effect is $O(\Delta)$ and is absorbed into the rate.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[higher-order derivatives]
For $k \ge 2$ the decoder applies $k$ integrations. First-order $\Sigma\Delta$
cancels one; the remaining $k-1$ turn the bounded residual into a drift of
order $T^{k-1}\Delta/2$. This is why the current encoder uses $k = 1$.
A $k$-th-order $\Sigma\Delta$ modulator would restore the $O(\Delta)$ bound
at the cost of more complex state.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Cross-entropy bound}

Let $X_t$ be the continuous next-step increment conditional on context $\mathrm{ctx}$,
with density $p(\cdot \mid \mathrm{ctx})$ and differential entropy
$h(X_t \mid \mathrm{ctx}) = -\int p \log p$.

\begin{proposition}[achievable CE]
\label{prop:ce}
In the fine-quantization limit $\Delta \to 0$, the expected cross-entropy of
the optimal autoregressive token predictor satisfies
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^\star(V) \;=\; h(X_t \mid \mathrm{ctx}) + \log V - \log(2D) \;+\; o(1).
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $\mathcal L^\star$ is linear in $\log V$ with slope $1$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
Standard quantized-entropy relation: for a continuous RV with density $p$ and
bin width $\Delta$,
\begin{equation}
H(\mathrm{bin}) = -\sum_b P(\mathrm{bin}{=}b) \log P(\mathrm{bin}{=}b) = h(X) - \log \Delta + o(1)
\qquad (\Delta \to 0).
\end{equation}
(See e.g.\ Cover \& Thomas, \emph{Elements of Information Theory}, \S 8.3.)
The conditional version replaces $X$ by $X \mid \mathrm{ctx}$. Substituting
$\Delta = 2D/(V-2)$ gives the stated formula.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[vocabulary-invariant skill]
\label{cor:skill}
Two models at vocabulary sizes $V_1 \neq V_2$ are comparable only through the
\emph{differential-entropy residual}
\begin{equation}
\tilde h(V) := \mathcal L(V) - \log V + \log(2D),
\end{equation}
which estimates $h(X_t \mid \mathrm{ctx})$ and is independent of $V$ at the rate.
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Empirical check}

On ETTh1 with seq\_len = 96, pred\_len = 96, trained for 2000 iters:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
$V$ & val CE (nats) & $\log V / \log 4$ & $\mathcal L(V) - \log V$ \\
\midrule
256 & 3.96 & $\log 256 = 5.545$ & $-1.585$ \\
64  & 2.51 & $\log 64  = 4.159$ & $-1.649$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

The predicted gap $\log(256/64) = 1.386$ nats matches the observed $1.45$ nats
to within $0.06$ --- the latter is the $\tilde h$ residual. Consistent with
Proposition~\ref{prop:ce}.

\section{Ordinal soft cross-entropy}
\label{sec:loss}

Standard cross-entropy treats the $V$ bins as nominal categories: a prediction
that is off by one bin is penalized identically to a prediction that is off by
fifty. This is a lie: bins are an ordinal index over a continuous scale, and
``off by one'' is almost right.

We replace the one-hot target $\mathbf 1_{i = y}$ with a discrete Gaussian
over bin indices:
\begin{equation}
p^{\text{soft}}_i(y; \sigma) \;\propto\; \exp\!\left(-\tfrac{(i - y)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right),
\qquad \sigma \ge 0,
\end{equation}
and train with standard soft-target cross-entropy
$\mathcal L_\sigma = -\sum_i p^{\text{soft}}_i \log q_i$, where $q = \mathrm{softmax}(\text{logits})$.
Taking $\sigma \to 0$ recovers ordinary cross-entropy with one-hot targets.

\paragraph{Why this matters for a $\Sigma\Delta$-quantized signal.}
A continuous value landing near a bin boundary is, modulo carry, assigned to
one of the two adjacent bins. The $\Sigma\Delta$ encoder can shift it either
way depending on the running accumulator. Training with a one-hot target
pretends this assignment is deterministic; soft targets acknowledge the
effectively stochastic tie-breaking. More broadly, any aleatoric component of
$p(X \mid \mathrm{ctx})$ makes the true conditional distribution over bins
already a smooth Gaussian-like shape, and label smoothing with a scale
$\sigma$ \emph{in bin units} is the ordinal equivalent of matching that
distribution.

\paragraph{Bin-space versus value-space.}
Because bin width $\Delta = 2D/V$, a constant value-space smoothing
$\sigma_{\text{val}} = \sigma \cdot \Delta$ requires $\sigma$ to scale
\emph{linearly in} $V$. A value of $\sigma$ tuned at $V = 64$ is under-scaled
at $V = 256$ by a factor of $4$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{M4 Hourly (short-term, global)}

Following the M4 leaderboard protocol: per-series $z$-score normalization,
one global model trained across all 414 Hourly series, forecast horizon
$H = 48$. Model: 2-layer GPT, $d = 64$, $h = 4$, $V = 256$, 20k iters, AdamW,
best-val checkpoint restored. Metric: sMAPE (smaller is better).

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
loss & sMAPE & MASE \\
\midrule
plain CE ($\sigma=0$)    & 18.58 & -- \\
soft-CE $\sigma = 1.0$  & 17.55 & 1.663 \\
soft-CE $\sigma = 2.0$  & \textbf{17.49} & \textbf{1.659} \\
soft-CE $\sigma = 3.0$  & 17.35 & 1.949 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

$\sigma = 2$ is the joint-metric optimum. At $\sigma = 3$ sMAPE continues to
improve but MASE degrades sharply --- a classic over-smoothing signature where
forecasts regress toward the conditional mean, which is rewarded under the
symmetric sMAPE but punished under the naive-seasonal-normalized MASE.
The one-point sMAPE gain at $\sigma = 1.0$ over plain CE is the core
qualitative finding: \emph{on a short-horizon benchmark with many short,
heterogeneous series, telling the model that ``off by one bin is almost
right'' is worth as much as a substantial architectural change.}

\subsection{ETTh1 (long-term, multivariate)}

iTransformer protocol: 7 channels, seq\_len = 96, pred\_len = 96, chronological
12/4/4-month split, StandardScaler from train. Single model at
$d = 128$, $h = 4$, 2 layers, 2000 iters, batch 32, AdamW.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
\toprule
config & MSE & MAE \\
\midrule
$V = 256$, plain CE                 & 0.9861 & 0.6056 \\
$V = 64$, plain CE                   & 0.9787 & 0.6030 \\
$V = 64$, soft-CE $\sigma = 1.0$     & 0.9806 & 0.6034 \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

ETTh1 is dominated by a capacity / data-fit floor at roughly $\mathrm{MSE} = 0.98$:
shrinking the vocabulary and applying soft targets each move the number by
within-noise amounts. The contrast with M4 suggests that the \emph{value} of
soft targets is concentrated in the data-starved regime where many short series
force the model to extrapolate conditional distributions without enough signal
to localize a single bin.

\section{Related work}

\textbf{Inverted/channel-mixing transformers.} iTransformer \cite{itransformer2024}
and PatchTST \cite{patchtst2023} remove the autoregressive head and regress the
entire horizon in one shot, which is the dominant design on long-horizon
benchmarks. Our model keeps the autoregressive GPT recipe unchanged; the
tokenization carries the inductive bias.

\textbf{Ordinal and label-smoothed classification.} Label smoothing
\cite{szegedy2016} distributes probability uniformly over non-target classes.
Distance-weighted variants (cumulative link models, ordinal softmax) exist in
the ordinal-regression literature. Gaussian soft targets specifically appear
in CORAL \cite{cao2020} and in diffusion-model score-matching of discretized
targets \cite{austin2021}. Our contribution is situating the smoothing scale
$\sigma$ in the vocabulary-scaled tokenization of a $\Sigma\Delta$ encoder.

\textbf{$\Sigma\Delta$ quantization.} First-order sigma--delta modulators are
a classical element of analog-to-digital conversion \cite{candy1992}; we apply
them as a differentiable-free preprocessing step in front of a transformer.

\section{Conclusion}

A decoder-only transformer trained autoregressively on derivative-plus-$\Sigma\Delta$-quantized
tokens attains competitive short-term and long-term forecasting without any
specialized head. The tokenization contract gives two closed-form vocabulary-
scaling laws; an ordinal soft cross-entropy matches the natural bin-wise
geometry and yields a $6\%$ relative sMAPE reduction on M4 Hourly without
architectural change.
